How neurosis looks through cybernetics & information-metabolism — and why metric-only thinking froze research

Short version: clinically, “neurosis” described a complex pattern of regulation — nested feedback loops, hierarchical control, and meaning-making — not a tidy checklist. Framing that complexity with cybernetics (feedback, requisite variety, levels of control) and with Antoni Kępiński’s information-metabolism makes the stakes clear: neurosis is a failure or narrowing of an organism’s capacity to process, transform and act on information from its world. When decision-makers (and funders, journals, diagnosis committees) fall into the McNamara fallacy — privileging what can be counted and discarding the rest — they systematically starve those complex, hard-to-measure research programs. That process was amplified by Cold-War institutional dynamics on both sides of the Iron Curtain and produced a decades-long “winter” for research into clinically rich, system-level phenomena. 


---

1) Two complementary lenses for what clinicians meant by “neurosis”

Cybernetics: control, feedback, levels

Cybernetics treats organisms and families as regulators that keep variables within acceptable ranges via feedback loops. Key ideas that map onto clinical neurosis:

Homeostatic loops and pathological set-points. Symptoms (panic attacks, compulsions, chronic anxiety) are signals in feedback loops that once helped stabilize the system but now maintain maladaptive equilibria.

Hierarchical/levels problems (logical types). Some clinical paradoxes are “messages at two levels” (the classic Bateson double bind): a patient cannot satisfy both a primary demand and a meta-demand, producing chronic disorganization of response repertoires. This makes the problem intrinsically contextual and relational, not reducible to one observable variable. 

Requisite variety. Ashby’s law says a regulator must have at least as much variety as the disturbances it must control. Neurosis can be read as the organism or environment lacking requisite variety: either the person’s behavioral/interpretive repertoire is too narrow, or the social environment generates disturbances of excessive variety (or the clinician’s tools lack variety). That’s why quick symptom counts miss the core: they don’t capture the needed repertoire. 


Information-metabolism: organisms as information processors

Antoni Kępiński’s information-metabolism frames psychic life as the ongoing exchange and transformation of information between organism and environment. Important clinical points:

Information quality matters, not just quantity. Meaning, intentionality, privileged context — the same “input” can be metabolized adaptively or pathologically depending on the system’s internal structure. 

Saturation, overload, and maladaptive filtering. Neurosis often looks like chronic maladaptive filtering: either too much noise, leading to hypervigilance and anxiety, or overly rigid filtering, leading to stereotypy and compulsions.

Temporal metabolism. Information must be integrated across time (narrative, history). Short snapshot measures miss the temporal transformations central to clinical change.


Putting the two together: neurosis = a systemic information-processing failure — where feedback, levels, repertoire and temporal integration break down. That is why clinicians found the term useful: it pointed to pattern, function, meaning and process, not just a bundle of present symptoms.

2) How the McNamara fallacy knocks out the things that matter for neurosis

The McNamara fallacy (deciding by what’s easily measured and ignoring the rest) undermines exactly the kinds of observations cybernetics and information-metabolism say are central:

It converts process into snapshots. Feedback dynamics, levels of control, and temporal integration require longitudinal, relational, qualitative and multi-modal measurement. Counting symptom checklist items once or scoring a scale weekly flattens trajectories and erases higher-order patterns. 

It privileges narrow endpoints that are trial-friendly. Randomized controlled trials and regulatory endpoints (e.g., symptom-scale drops at 8 weeks) reward interventions that change short-term counts. They reward manipulable variables at the expense of complex targets like narrative coherence, relational reciprocity, or the patient’s information-metabolic capacity. 

It collapses levels. When a checklist item is treated as the disorder itself (reification), clinicians stop looking for the higher-level organizational processes that produced it — the very thing cybernetics says you must see to intervene usefully.


So the fallacy doesn’t merely bias research; it changes the object of study. What remained visible were the signals that fit neatly into psychometrics and trial design; what was lost were the regulatory narratives, family-system patterns, and subtle self-organizing dynamics.

3) Why the Cold War made that narrowing systemic and long-lived

United States: operationalization, reliability, and market forces

In the U.S. context, the shift to operationalized, atheoretical diagnostic criteria (famously consolidated in ) was driven by a real need for reliability — different clinicians agreeing on diagnoses — and by the rise of standardized clinical trials. The DSM-III project removed etiological/psychodynamic terms like “neurosis” and redistributed their phenomena into discrete, descriptive categories to enable research and reimbursement. That made psychiatry more testable, but it also made the field more hospitable to short, measurable outcomes and pharmaceutical development — a perfect breeding ground for the McNamara fallacy. 

(Inline entity: American Psychiatric Association was the institutional locus for the DSM work.)

Soviet bloc: ideology, reductionism and suppression of alternative frameworks

In the Soviet context, psychiatry was shaped by different pressures: ideological conformity, political control of diagnostic categories, and periods of biological or Pavlovian orthodoxy that marginalized heterodox, context-sensitive frameworks. Political instrumentalization of psychiatry (and the enforcement of certain “scientific” paradigms) meant that clinical traditions emphasizing psychoanalytic, relational, or open-systems complexity were often marginalized or suspect — again removing institutional support for research that treats neurosis as a complex regulation problem. 

Net effect: two different funnels, same outcome

Although the drivers differed, both superpowers produced incentives that favored simplification:

U.S. incentives: inter-rater reliability, clinical trials, pharma, insurance — all of which reward measurable endpoints. 

Soviet incentives: ideological conformity, biologistic narratives, and institutional suppression of heterodox models — all of which deprioritized relational/systemic inquiry. 


The result: large swathes of research, funding, training and publication moved away from system-level, cybernetic and information-metabolic questions about neurosis — a scientific “winter” for those approaches.

4) Concrete consequences of that “winter”

Fewer longitudinal, systems-oriented studies (family interaction patterns, narrative integration, multi-level feedback) received funding or publication outlets. 

Therapeutic attention narrowed toward protocolized brief treatments and medication that moved symptom counts but often left the larger regulatory dysfunction intact. 

Loss of conceptual tools: terms that pointed to function (e.g., “neurosis” with its psychodynamic, interpersonal and developmental connotations) atrophied from mainstream training, making it harder for clinicians to notice and study systemic problems.

5) How a cybernetic + information-metabolic research program would differ (and how to revive it)

If you take the frameworks seriously, research and clinical practice change in predictable ways:

Measure processes, not just states. Repeated, time-series measurement (ecological momentary assessment, interaction coding, physiological loops) to map feedback dynamics.

Increase requisite variety in interventions. Therapies that expand patients’ repertoires (skills, narrative frames, relational moves) rather than only suppressing symptom counts. Ashby’s lesson: the controller (therapist, system) must match the variety of disturbances. 

Multi-level endpoints. Include measures of narrative coherence, social network function, allostatic load, and patient-reported meaning/agency in trials — not only symptom scales.

Mixed methods and complex-systems analytics. Combine qualitative case work (to capture meaning and temporal integration) with dynamical systems modelling, network analysis, and cybernetic interventions (e.g., altering feedback loops in family systems). 

Institutional changes. Funding calls and journals should explicitly reward studies that accept complexity (longer timeframes, mixed methods), and training should reintroduce conceptual tools for system-level thinking.

6) A short clinical vignette to make it concrete

A patient with chronic “anxiety” comes with panic attacks, relationship avoidance, and a rigid self-criticism loop. A metric-only approach: administer a GAD scale, give a short CBT protocol, measure symptom reduction at 8 weeks. A cybernetic+information-metabolic approach: map triggers, relational feedbacks (partner responses), the patient’s meaning maps (why panic matters), and the timescale of escalation and recovery; design interventions that change immediate regulation (breathing, exposure), meta-level learning (deutero-learning to reframe the rules of interaction), and social context (partner coaching). The latter asks for more varied measurement and longer follow-up — the very things the McNamara fallacy and Cold-War incentives made rarer.
