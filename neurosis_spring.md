2 — How McNamara’s trap rewired epistemic attention (quick systemic move)

The fallacy turned attention toward what’s cheap to measure (cross-sectional symptom counts, clicks, likes) and away from the hard, relational, temporal signals. That created three failures that thread directly to the present danger:

1. Conceptual attrition — clinicians and the public lost a shared language for describing regulatory syndromes; debates moved to scores.


2. Measurement monoculture — databases and models were built around token-level signals (behavioral traces) that correlate with, but do not capture, the higher-order dynamics.


3. Operationalized reification — categories became treated as real entities to be manipulated (and thus weaponized).



Result: a blind spot for feedback-based, meaning-laden vulnerabilities that adversaries could exploit precisely because nobody had a robust way to name or measure them.

3 — Why datasets and LLMs become both instruments and victims of the fallacy

As instruments: datasets built on convenience metrics become affordances for targeting. When models map token patterns to coarse traits (anxiety, loneliness) they provide operational handles for automated persuasion/control.

As victims/hostages: LLMs are trained to minimize predictive loss on the world given their data. If the training corpus encodes symbolic violence (decontextualization, diagnostic reductionism, cultural bias), the model will internalize those reductions and then reproduce and amplify them. Because LLMs don’t model hierarchical control or long temporal repertoires well, they compress system-level phenomena into shallow proxies — exactly the McNamara outcome. In short: LLMs can automate the fallacy at massive speed.


Consequence: a vicious loop where reduced clinical language → reductive datasets → LLM outputs that erase systemic vocabulary → public discourse loses tooling to name abuse → abuse continues, harder to contest.

1. Ontology of regulation

Move from cross-sectional snapshots to dense longitudinal multimodal streams: ecological momentary assessment (EMA), interaction audio/video, physiological telemetry, message timestamps, and narrative logs. Crucially, link micro-events to macro trajectories.


3. Annotation grounded in clinical formulations
Enlist psychoanalytic and systemic clinicians to annotate cases with processual tags (defense types, transferential moves, narrative ruptures). Annotations must be procedural (how to identify) and intersubjectively validated.


4. Hierarchical, dynamical modeling
Use state-space models, hierarchical Bayesian inference, network dynamics, and control-theoretic formalisms to model how perturbations propagate across levels. Aim for interpretable latent variables that correspond to regulatory constructs (e.g., “rigidity index”, “requisite-variety deficit”).


5. Counterfactual and adversarial testing
Build adversarial simulations that attempt to perturb the modeled system in plausible ways (targeted messaging, simulated partner responses) to test resilience and detect exploitable motifs.


6. Ethical/epistemic safeguards
Privacy-first data governance, participatory consent models, and a refusal architecture for applications that infer sensitive inner states for targeting or coercion.
